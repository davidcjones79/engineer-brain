01 — The big idea

Two types of problem-solving. Engineers only know one.

Every normal human has a simple process: identify the problem, fix it, move on with life. Engineers identified the problem, then identified seventeen sub-problems, architected a framework, wrote a spec, and missed the original deadline by four sprints.

The normal way

Light switch is broken

You call an electrician. The electrician fixes it. You turn the light on. It works. You go about your day. Total time elapsed: 45 minutes. Emotional damage: none.

The engineer way

Light switch is broken

You research IoT-enabled smart switches. You order a Raspberry Pi. You write a custom firmware. You set up Home Assistant. Your spouse leaves. The original light switch is still broken. Total time elapsed: 3 weekends. Emotional damage: catastrophic.

02 — Meet the species
🧠

Homo Engineerus

Kingdom: Caffeinata · Class: Overthinkus · Order: Debuggeriformes

Known for an extraordinary ability to make simple things complicated, then complaining about the complexity. Identified in the wild by their mechanical keyboard, strong opinions about tabs vs. spaces, and the haunted look of someone who just reviewed a pull request.

Age 7

Corrects a teacher's rounding error. Gets first taste of being technically correct — the most intoxicating kind of correct. It becomes a lifestyle.

Age 14

Builds first website. It looks terrible. Spends three months optimizing the load time to 0.4 seconds. Nobody visits it. Considers this a win because "the architecture is solid."

Age 22

Graduates with an engineering degree and $80K in student loans. Immediately spends $3,200 on a home lab setup. Cannot explain this decision to their parents. Or themselves.

Age 30

Has opinions about fourteen programming languages, three Linux distros, and the correct way to make coffee (pour-over, obviously, with water at exactly 96°C, measured with a thermocouple they soldered themselves).

Age 45

Finally admits the real architecture was the friends they debugged with along the way. Still refuses to use a GUI when a terminal command exists. Will die on this hill.

03 — How it works

The Engineer Decision Process, with no whiteboard available.

An engineer's brain processes every decision through an elaborate pipeline that would be impressive if it didn't take 4x longer than just doing the thing.

1

Encounter a simple problem

Something doesn't work. A normal person would fix it. The engineer's brain immediately activates a threat-assessment protocol that classifies the problem by severity, root cause probability, and whether it could be solved "more elegantly."

2

Research phase (endless)

Open 47 browser tabs. Read three Stack Overflow threads. Watch a conference talk from 2019. Discover a new tool that "solves this perfectly." Spend two hours learning that tool. Realize it doesn't actually solve the original problem.

3

Architecture astronautics

Design a solution so abstract and future-proof it could survive a nuclear apocalypse, a database migration, and a reorg — simultaneously. The original problem was a broken link on page 4 of the website.

4

Implementation & despair

Begin building. Encounter an edge case. Redesign everything. Encounter another edge case. Consult a rubber duck. The duck has no answers. Question career choices. Push to production at 2am. It works. No one knows why. Never touch it again.

Multiply any time estimate they give you by π (3.14). This is known as the Engineer Uncertainty Principle.

Because to an engineer, every question is actually seventeen questions wearing a trench coat. "Should we use React?" depends on the team size, the bundle size, the legacy codebase, the deployment target, whether Mercury is in retrograde, and at least four other variables they'll need a whiteboard session to enumerate. They're not being evasive. Their brain literally cannot produce a simple answer. It's a hardware limitation.

This is a harmful stereotype and we won't perpetuate it. Engineers have *excellent* social skills — with other engineers. They can bond over shared hatred of a programming language, debate architecture patterns for 3 hours straight, and forge lifelong friendships over a mutual appreciation for mechanical keyboards. It's the rest of humanity they struggle with, mostly because the rest of humanity keeps saying things like "just make it work" without providing acceptance criteria.
